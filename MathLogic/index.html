3.1 Weaker/Stronger Predicates

  1. Simple example: x > 7 implies x > 1.
  2. Consider: P implies Q (also written as P ⇒ Q). We say that P is stronger than Q. Alt: Q is weaker than P.

3.2 A Model for a WFF

  1. Well-formed-formulae P and Q, in general, have free variables, x, y, z, … Consider a particular assignment A of values for each of x, y, z that makes P come out True. A will also make Q True.
  2. In the context of programming, we can refer to this A as a "state". In logic, this is known as a model.
  3. Let C(Q) be the collection of such assignments that make Q come out as True. Similarly, C(P).
  4. Then, C(P) is a subset of C(Q), if and when P is stronger than Q.
  5. If C(P) = C(Q), P is equivalent to Q (also written as P ⇔ Q, or P ≡ Q).
